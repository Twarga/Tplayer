# Tplayer Brand Guide

Tplayer is a desktop music player for people who want their own library to feel fast, beautiful, and personal. The product should feel like a serious music app, not a utility window and not a streaming-service clone.

## Positioning

One-line pitch:

> Tplayer is a polished desktop music player for local libraries, YouTube imports, and focused listening.

Short description:

> Tplayer turns a local music folder into a refined daily player with fast playback, clean queue control, YouTube importing, Last.fm scrobbling, MPRIS support, and a bold dark interface built around album art.

What it is:

- A local-first desktop music player.
- A polished replacement for daily listening when the user owns or imports their music.
- A focused library, queue, and playback experience.
- A music app with strong visual identity and motion, not a plain file browser.

What it is not:

- Not a Spotify clone.
- Not a streaming catalog.
- Not a social music network.
- Not a dashboard full of filler sections.
- Not a generic Electron demo.

## Product Promise

Tplayer should make a personal music library feel immediate, elegant, and alive.

Every public page, screenshot, and release note should support three ideas:

- `Own your listening`: local files and imported tracks stay first-class.
- `Move fast`: search, queue, seek, and playback should feel direct.
- `Look intentional`: the interface should feel dramatic, musical, and refined.

## Voice

Use direct, confident language. Avoid hype that the app cannot prove yet.

Preferred wording:

- "local-first"
- "desktop music player"
- "YouTube import"
- "queue control"
- "warm dark interface"
- "built for focused listening"

Avoid:

- "Spotify-like"
- "AI-powered"
- "revolutionary"
- "ultimate"
- "all-in-one platform"
- "stream any song"

## Visual Direction

The public visual identity should match the app direction:

- Dark editorial music mood.
- Warm amber/gold accent, not purple-first.
- Large album art and strong typography.
- Wide cinematic layouts.
- Thin dividers and layered light, not nested rounded cards.
- Motion that feels musical and deliberate.

Design references should be interpreted as mood and structure, not copied literally.

## Color Direction

Core palette:

- Ink: `#070606`
- Charcoal: `#100d0b`
- Walnut: `#21180e`
- Amber: `#d6a84f`
- Gold: `#f2cf7a`
- Bone: `#f4efe6`
- Muted text: `#a9a098`

Usage:

- Use amber/gold for active states, playback focus, and primary calls to action.
- Use bone for primary text.
- Use muted text for metadata.
- Avoid large purple surfaces in marketing and repo visuals.

## Screenshot Direction

Screenshots should show the app as a daily music player:

- Home with now-playing context.
- Library with real tracks.
- YouTube import with clear search/import states.
- A visible bottom player and seek bar.

Use real-looking music metadata, but avoid copyrighted album art in public assets unless permission is clear. For release screenshots, prefer local test artwork, generated artwork, or original assets.

## Landing Page Direction

The landing page should feel like a music product, not a startup template.

Required sections:

- Hero: app screenshot, one-line pitch, download CTA.
- Why Tplayer: local-first, queue control, imports, integrations.
- Interface story: show the dark editorial design and playback focus.
- Downloads: Linux AppImage and Windows build.
- Developer section: clone, install, run, build.

Avoid generic feature-card grids as the main visual structure. Use rhythm, artwork, screenshots, and strong sections instead.
